# Saying Yes to Every Social Invitation for a Month

My default is no. Not unfriendly no — just efficient no. Plans require
energy, energy is finite, I'd usually rather be home. If I'm honest, I'd
also say I'm pretty good at predicting which invitations will be worth it
and which won't.

Except I started noticing something: I was almost always wrong. The things
I'd dreaded turned out fine. The spontaneous dinners I nearly skipped were
often the best evenings of the month. The pattern was consistent enough
that I started to question whether my prediction instinct was actually
trustworthy at all.

So I ran an experiment.

## The Rules

For one month, I said yes to every social invitation. Dinners, events,
coffees, spontaneous plans, things I would normally have immediately
declined. One hard rule: no cancelling. If I said yes, I went.

I logged how I felt before each one and after each one.

## What the Numbers Looked Like

Before: I dreaded the majority of them. Anywhere from mild reluctance to
genuine "I really don't want to do this."

After: I enjoyed almost all of them. Something like 30 out of 31.

That's a significant gap. My brain was consistently predicting awfulness
that never materialised.

## The Dread Was the Lie

This was the central finding, and it's a strange one to sit with.

The dread felt legitimate. It had reasons. I'd be tired, it would run
late, I wouldn't know anyone, I'd rather finish what I was working on. But
these reasons, however plausible they sounded, turned out to be mostly
fiction. The reality of being somewhere with people was almost always
better than the anticipatory version.

I don't fully understand why the brain does this. Part of it might be that
effort feels larger in advance than in the moment. Part of it might be that
I was optimizing for an imagined version of the evening instead of the
actual one. But whatever the mechanism, the dread wasn't reliable
information. It was noise.

## But Some No's Are Load-Bearing

By week three I was genuinely exhausted. Not unhappy — I'd had a good
month — but I was running on fumes. Turns out some of my no's exist for a
reason. Not because the event would be bad, but because I needed the
evening to myself. Rest is also a real need.

The lesson isn't "say yes more." It's something more precise: **trust
yourself less when saying no in advance.** The no I give before I've
actually thought about it, the reflexive one, the one based on vague
reluctance — that one should be interrogated. The deliberate no, the one
where I actually know I need the time, that one is probably fine.

## What I Kept

I now have one rule: if I want to say no, I sleep on it first. Not every
time — that would be exhausting in its own way — but for anything where I
notice the refusal is reflexive rather than considered.

It's a small filter but it's already changed a few evenings I'd otherwise
have missed.
