# Morning vs. Evening Journaling

I've always journaled at night. Before bed, I'd write a few paragraphs
about the day — what happened, what I felt, what I was still thinking
about. It felt natural to process the day at the end of it.

But I kept reading about morning pages. Writers and creators swearing by
them. The idea that writing before the world gets to you, before your
brain is crowded, produces something different and maybe truer.

I decided to test it properly.

## The Setup

Six weeks journaling in the morning, right after waking up. Six weeks
journaling in the evening, same as before. Same rough prompt each time:
*what's on my mind?* No rules about length or format.

I noted how I felt before and after writing, and whether I felt like I had
something to say.

## The Difference

Morning writing was rawer. I'd write things I wouldn't have written in
the evening — half-formed thoughts, anxieties before the day had shaped
them into something more managed. Sometimes these were interesting.
Sometimes they were just noise.

Evening writing was more processed. I'd already lived the day and had
opinions about it. The entries were more structured and, honestly, more
readable. But they were also more edited. I was writing about what
happened, not what was happening.

I also wrote longer entries in the morning. This surprised me. I expected
to have more to say after a full day. Instead, an empty morning seemed to
produce more words than a full evening.

## The Problem with the Experiment

Halfway through I realised the experiment had a flaw: my life isn't
consistent week to week. Stressful weeks produce different evening entries
than calm ones. A bad night's sleep changes morning writing entirely.
There were too many confounders to make a clean comparison.

I changed the question partway through. Instead of asking *which is
better*, I started asking *what do I actually write about at each time of
day?* Morning entries were more about fear and anticipation. Evening
entries were more about events and people.

That turned out to be more interesting than a verdict.

## What I Actually Concluded

I went back to evenings. Not because they're better, but because they suit
how I actually want to use the journal — to process, not to excavate.
But I occasionally do a morning entry when something is sitting heavily
before the day starts. That's the right use of it for me.

The real finding: the time of day changes what you write about more than
how well you write. That's worth knowing.
