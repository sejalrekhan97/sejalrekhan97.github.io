# Walking After Every Meal for Three Weeks

I've always heard that a short walk after eating is good for you. Blood
sugar, digestion, energy — people cite all kinds of reasons. But I'd
never actually tested it in any systematic way. I just ate lunch, sat back
down at my desk, and waited for the 3pm wall to hit me.

So I decided to find out.

## The Experiment

For three weeks, I walked for at least 30 minutes after every meal.
Breakfast, lunch, dinner — all of them, when I could manage it. I kept a
short note each day on two things: how I felt around 3pm, and whether the
afternoon slump arrived, and if so, how bad.

I compared this against my previous three weeks where I did nothing
different after eating.

## What I Expected

Honestly, I expected the walks to help a bit but mostly to feel like a
chore. I'd have to carve out time, I'd feel guilty if I missed one, and
the benefit would probably be marginal.

I also fully expected to abandon the habit by week two.

## What Actually Happened

The afternoon slump didn't disappear. I want to be clear about that —
it still came. But it shifted in character. It went from feeling like
hitting a wall to feeling more like a gentle dip. The difference between
"I cannot function" and "I'm a little tired."

The bigger surprise was this: the walk itself became the part of the day I
looked forward to most. Not the health benefit. Not the productivity
boost. Just the 30 minutes of being outside and not doing anything in
particular.

By week three, I'd stopped thinking of it as an experiment and just
started doing it.

## What I Kept

The lunch walk. Every day now, and I don't track it or think about it
much. The breakfast and dinner walks fell away — too early, too late, too
easy to skip. But the post-lunch one stuck.

The lesson I actually took from this: the benefit I was measuring (energy
in the afternoon) wasn't the reason I kept the habit. I kept it because it
became a small thing I genuinely wanted to do. That might be the only way
any habit actually sticks.
