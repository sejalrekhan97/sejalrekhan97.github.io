# No Phone for the First Hour: Week One Notes

*This one is ongoing. I'm writing it as I go.*

---

The experiment started because of a feeling I couldn't quite name. Every
morning, before I was properly awake, I'd be in my phone. Not looking for
anything in particular — just moving between apps on a kind of autopilot.
By the time I made coffee I'd already been online for 20 minutes.

And the mornings felt crowded. Not because of anything I'd read, not
usually. Just from the switching. The constant small redirections of
attention before I'd had a chance to have any attention at all.

I wanted to test whether that feeling was real or imagined.

## The Rule

Phone stays in another room until after breakfast and a short walk.
Nothing fancy. I track my mood and focus at 10am each day on a simple
three-point scale.

## Days 1–4

The urge is reflexive. That's the first thing I noticed. It's not that I
consciously wanted to check something — it was more like my hand was
already moving. Reaching toward where the phone usually sits before I'd
thought about it.

This was surprising. I'd thought of myself as someone who uses their phone
deliberately. Apparently not always.

Days 3 and 4 were harder. I think because the novelty of the experiment
had worn off and the habit-groove of checking hadn't yet.

## Days 5–6

Something shifted. I don't have a better word for it than "settled."

The mornings feel less busy. Not quieter exactly — I still have thoughts
and things to do — but the texture is different. There's less of that
feeling of being pulled in small directions before you've decided where
you're going.

I also noticed I'm hungrier at breakfast. I'm not sure what that means,
but it's been consistent.

## What I Think Is Happening

My original hypothesis was about bad news — that reading upsetting things
first thing was setting a negative tone for the day. But that's not quite
right. Even on mornings where I'd only looked at mundane things, the
crowded feeling was there.

The anxiety, if that's the right word, isn't from content. It's from the
switching. The phone creates a mode of attention — fractured, responsive,
outward-facing — that takes a while to dissolve. And if you start the
morning in that mode, you carry it for a while.

The first hour without it seems to be enough to start differently.
