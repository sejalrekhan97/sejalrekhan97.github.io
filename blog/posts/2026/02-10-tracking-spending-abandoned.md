# Why I Stopped Tracking My Spending on Day 41

I want to write about an experiment I didn't finish.

There's a version of this write-up that's tidy and conclusive — the one
where I complete the 60 days, extract the insights, and package them
neatly. But I stopped on day 41, and I think the stopping itself is the
finding worth writing about.

## What I Was Trying to Do

The idea was simple: track every rupee I spent for 60 days, without any
budgeting rules. Just observation. The hypothesis was that awareness alone
would change my behavior — that seeing the numbers would surface patterns
I couldn't see without them.

## What Happened in the First Three Weeks

The tracking worked. I noticed things I'd been ignoring. Small purchases
that added up quietly. Categories I'd been mentally undercounting. There
were a couple of genuine surprises — one of them meaningfully changed
something I was doing.

This part felt good. It felt like the experiment was working.

## Where It Started to Break

Somewhere around day 25, the relationship changed.

I wasn't tracking my spending to understand it anymore. I was tracking it
because I felt I had to. And the act of logging had started to carry a
weight it hadn't before. Not guilt exactly — more like anticipatory
judgment. Before buying something, I'd think about whether I wanted to
write it down.

That's not awareness. That's surveillance.

The things I stopped buying weren't things I'd decided weren't worth it.
They were things I didn't want to confess to the spreadsheet. That's a
different kind of decision-making, and I don't think it's a better one.

## Why I Stopped

By day 41 I was dreading the log. Dreading a spreadsheet, which is an
absurd thing to feel, but there it was. The experiment had flipped from
"interesting to observe" to "actively making me feel bad about ordinary
things."

I closed the spreadsheet and stopped.

## What I Actually Learned

The experiment worked, and then it stopped working, and the transition
between those two states is the most interesting thing I found.

Observation changed my behavior. But behavior changed for reasons I
hadn't expected and wouldn't have endorsed if I'd been thinking clearly.
The tool I'd built to create awareness had, at some point, become a tool
for self-criticism.

I think this might be true of a lot of tracking: there's a window where
the data is illuminating, and then it crosses into something else. The
question is whether you notice when you've crossed it.

I didn't notice until day 41. Next time, earlier.

## What I'd Do Differently

A shorter experiment. Two or three weeks of tracking is probably enough to
surface the real patterns. Beyond that, you're not learning more — you're
just living with a ledger.

I'd also separate the observation period from any decisions. Track first,
review once at the end, then decide. Don't let the tracking itself become
the intervention.
