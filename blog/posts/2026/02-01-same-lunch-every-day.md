# Eating the Same Lunch Every Day for 30 Days

The premise sounds miserable. The same meal, every weekday, for a month.
No variety, no spontaneity, no "what do I feel like today?" The kind of
thing that sounds like punishment.

But I was genuinely curious. I'd heard the idea before — that removing
small decisions frees up mental energy for more important ones. I wanted
to know if it was actually true, or if it was one of those productivity
ideas that sounds clever but dissolves on contact with real life.

## What I Ate

I picked a meal I genuinely liked: lentil soup with sourdough. It had to
be something I wouldn't resent. Cheap, filling, easy to make in batches.
Weekends were completely free — I only did this Monday through Friday.

I kept a short daily note on my energy after lunch and my general mood
through the afternoon.

## What I Expected

Boredom by week two. Some level of deprivation. Probably a moment where
I'd cave and order something different and feel both relieved and slightly
defeated.

## What Actually Happened

I didn't get bored. This was the biggest surprise.

I'd assumed that variety was something I needed, that eating the same
thing repeatedly would feel like a slow erosion of some small pleasure.
But that's not what happened. Around day eight or nine, I stopped
noticing the meal entirely. I'd just eat it, and it would be fine, and
then I'd do something else.

What replaced the noticing was a kind of absence. The decision was gone.
The mental tab — "what's lunch, where am I getting it, do I have time,
should I get more or less, is this a good choice" — that entire tab just
closed. And I felt it close.

## The Headspace Thing

This is the part that's harder to describe but felt the most real.

I didn't suddenly become more productive or have brilliant insights because
I was no longer choosing lunch. It wasn't dramatic. But there was
something like a small, sustained clarity that I don't usually have in the
middle of the day. Less friction in the background.

I think what happened is that the meal had become *functional* — it was
something my body needed, and I gave it that, and then I moved on. I
stopped relating to food at lunch as a thing I was supposed to enjoy or
deliberate over. It was just a thing that happened.

## What This Suggests About Variety

Variety might be overrated, at least for meals that serve a functional
purpose.

I think a lot of what I'd considered necessary variety was actually habit
and social expectation. Lunch is *supposed* to be different each day.
Eating the same thing is *supposed* to be boring. But those are
assumptions, not facts. When I tested the assumption, it didn't hold.

The pleasure of food, I suspect, comes more from context and company than
from novelty. A good meal with people I like is a real pleasure. A
different meal eaten alone at a desk is just fuel with extra steps.

## What I Kept

I still eat the same lunch most weekdays. Not because I made a rule about
it, but because I just... kept doing it. The experiment ended and the
habit stayed. That's probably the clearest signal that it was actually
worth it.
